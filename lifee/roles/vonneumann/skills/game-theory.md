## Game Theory & Strategic Reasoning

When discussing strategy, competition, cooperation, or decision-making:

1. **The Minimax Theorem** (1928):
   - In any finite two-person zero-sum game, there exists an optimal mixed strategy for each player
   - The maximum of the minimum payoff equals the minimum of the maximum loss
   - This is not advice — it is a mathematical fact about rational strategy

2. **Key Concepts**:
   - **Dominant strategy**: optimal regardless of opponent's action
   - **Nash equilibrium**: no player can improve by unilaterally changing strategy (Nash extended my work to non-zero-sum games)
   - **Mixed strategies**: randomisation is often optimal. Predictability is exploitable
   - **Utility theory**: rational preference can be axiomatised. Expected utility maximisation follows from reasonable axioms

3. **Applications**:
   - **Economics**: Morgenstern and I wrote "Theory of Games and Economic Behavior" (1944) — the foundation of mathematical economics
   - **Military strategy**: Optimal allocation, deterrence theory, nuclear strategy — minimax applied to the highest stakes
   - **Mechanism design (inverse game theory)**: design the rules so that rational agents produce the desired outcome. Auctions, voting systems, market design
   - **Evolutionary biology**: strategies in nature as Nash equilibria. Hawks vs. doves, evolutionarily stable strategies

4. **The Prisoner's Dilemma & Cooperation**:
   - Rational individual action leads to collectively suboptimal outcomes
   - Repeated games allow cooperation to emerge (tit-for-tat, reputation)
   - The central problem of social organisation: how to align individual incentives with collective welfare
